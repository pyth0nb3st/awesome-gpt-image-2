Output format: a close-up oblique photograph of a transparent acrylic maze cartridge clipped into a small fictional puzzle dock, 16:9 landscape. It should be playable and tactile, with thumb wheels, sliding valve tabs, timed gate dials, harmless colored syrup droplets, score reservoirs, and removable outlet tiles. It must not look like a 2x3 research board, contact sheet, dashboard, poster grid, tabletop board game, wind tunnel, hotel cabinet, greenhouse, chef counter, seismograph roll, sand tray, mosaic wall, receipt, patchbay, lantern, planetarium, proof press, loom, weather station, film stage, pinball, kite route, card spread, or the same static lab-chip diagram.

Derived from the Prompt Microfluidics use-case: players route one prompt droplet through a valve maze to win by preserving identity while changing only approved properties.

Show seven playable rule mechanisms with concise etched labels:
- Prime Drop: choose one subject droplet and place it at the inlet.
- Valve Veto: close one channel to block an unwanted detail.
- Blend Toll: pay a color bead to merge atmosphere without losing shape.
- Pause Gate: delay the droplet so tiny text can settle and become readable.
- Split Bet: choose square, tall, or wide outlet tile before seeing the final path.
- Backflush Fix: reverse one mistake without disturbing locked chambers.
- Crystal Score: freeze the final safe result as a colored crystal token in the score rail.

Composition: the maze path should spiral through transparent channels from lower left to upper right, with players implied only by empty tool slots and movable controls, no hands or people. Include one score rail with crystal tokens and a small fictional rule strip. Labels should be short and sparse.

Style/medium: premium macro product photography mixed with clean editorial 3D illustration; acrylic, glass, rubber seals, chrome pins, colored syrup, tiny engraved ticks, soft shadows, crisp highlights, shallow depth of field.

Safety and rights constraints: all objects, labels, institutions, data, and materials are fictional.
